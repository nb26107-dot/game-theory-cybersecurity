# 🟢 Chapter 1.2: Zero-Sum vs. Non-Zero-Sum Games in Cybersecurity

In game theory, interactions between attackers and defenders are classified based on how gains and losses are allocated. Understanding this distinction helps defenders choose the correct incident response and mitigation strategies.

---

## 🟥 1. Zero-Sum Games

In a *Zero-Sum Game*, the total gains and losses of all players always equal zero. This means that **any absolute gain for the Attacker is an absolute loss for the Defender**. There is no room for negotiation or win-win scenarios.

$$Gain_{Attacker} + Loss_{Defender} = 0$$

### 🛡️ Characteristics & Use Cases
* **Intellectual Property (IP) Theft:** If a competitor or a state-sponsored APT steals your company's proprietary source code or blueprints, they gain a $100\%$ competitive advantage, and you lose your exclusivity by $100\%$.
* **Website Defacement:** The attacker gains notoriety and bragging rights (+1), while your organization's reputation drops by the exact same margin (-1).

### 🎯 Defender's Strategy
In zero-sum scenarios, the defender's ultimate goal must be **Total Prevention**. Since any slip-up is a direct victory for the enemy, defenders must strictly enforce *Zero Trust Architecture*.

---

## 🟨 2. Non-Zero-Sum Games

In a *Non-Zero-Sum Game*, one party's gain does not necessarily translate into an equivalent loss for the other. There are scenarios where **both parties can lose (Lose-Lose)**, or where a compromise/mutual cost-mitigation can be achieved.

### 🛡️ Characteristics & Use Cases: Ransomware Attacks
Ransomware is the textbook definition of a non-zero-sum cyber game. Let's analyze the strategic outcomes:

1. **Scenario A (Defender Pays the Ransom):** * The *Attacker* gains financial profit but expends effort/risk providing the decryption key.
   * The *Defender* suffers financial loss but "gains" by restoring business operations quickly, avoiding catastrophic downtime.
2. **Scenario B (Defender Refuses to Pay & Restores from Backups):**
   * The *Attacker* earns $0$ financial payoff, losing the time and infrastructure costs spent on the breach.
   * The *Defender* saves the ransom money but still incurs significant operational costs and time delays during system recovery.

### 🎯 Defender's Strategy
In non-zero-sum environments, the defensive playbook shifts from pure prevention to **Risk Management and Cyber Resilience**. The focus is on minimizing the *blast radius* of an inevitable attack.

## 📊 Strategic Comparison Matrix for the Blue Team

| Dimension | Zero-Sum Game | Non-Zero-Sum Game |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Nature of Interaction** | Pure conflict; zero compromise. | Mixed motives; room for damage control. |
| **Primary Security Focus** | Confidentiality (Absolute data protection).| Availability & Integrity (Business continuity). |
| **Threat Examples** | Cyber espionage, state-sponsored data theft. | Ransomware, DDoS attacks, mass data leaks. |
| **Success Metric** | Attack completely thwarted. | Operational financial loss minimized. |
