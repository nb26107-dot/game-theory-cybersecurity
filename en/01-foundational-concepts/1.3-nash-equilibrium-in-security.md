# 🟢 Chapter 1.3: Nash Equilibrium in Cybersecurity

In the real world, neither the *Defender* nor the *Attacker* can change strategies without incurring costs. A **Nash Equilibrium** is a state in game theory where both players have chosen their optimal strategy, and no player can benefit by changing their strategy unilaterally.

For a *Defender*, finding the Nash Equilibrium helps predict the worst-case rational scenario of the attacker, preventing over-spending on redundant defenses.

---

## 🎲 Case Study: The "Patching vs. Exploitation" Game

Let's model a tactical interaction between a **Defender** (choosing to audit/install a security update: *Patch*, or ignore it: *No Patch*) and an **Attacker** (choosing to *Attack* or *No Attack*).

### 📊 Payoff Matrix
Here is the utility matrix showing rewards/penalties in the format `(Defender, Attacker)`:

| Defender \ Attacker | Attack | No Attack |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Patch** | $(-2, -5)$ [Point A] | $(-2, 0)$ [Point B] |
| **No Patch** | $(-10, +8)$ [Point C] | $(0, 0)$ [Point D] |

#### 🔍 Utility Analysis:
* **Cost of Patching (-2):** Deploying patches requires time, testing, and temporary service disruption.
* **Cost of Attack (-5):** Attacking a hardened, fully-patched system wastes the attacker's time and risks exposing their tools/IP addresses.
* **Infiltration Cost/Gain (-10 / +8):** If the defender neglects security (*No Patch*) and the attacker strikes, the defender loses heavily (-10), and the attacker gains valuable data (+8).

---

## 🏛️ Finding the Equilibrium

Let's analyze the logical flow of both players:
1. If the Defender chooses **No Patch**, the Attacker will **Attack** (preferring +8 over 0) -> leads to **Point C** $(-10, +8)$.
2. At Point C, the Defender suffers a massive loss (-10), so they pivot to **Patch** to reduce their loss to $-2$ -> leads to **Point A** $(-2, -5)$.
3. At Point A, the Attacker realizes that striking a patched system results in a loss of $-5$. They pivot to **No Attack** (preferring 0 over -5) -> leads to **Point B** $(-2, 0)$.
4. At Point B, the Defender thinks, *"Why am I paying a patching cost of -2 if there are no attacks?"* They pivot back to **No Patch** -> leads to **Point D** $(0, 0)$.

### 🌀 The Reality: Mixed Strategy Nash Equilibrium
As you can see, there is no single stable point (*Pure Strategy Nash Equilibrium*). Cyber security is a fluid cat-and-mouse game. 

The solution relies on a **Mixed Strategy**, where probability mathematics dictates how frequently a defender should audit systems randomly, and how often an attacker will attempt an exploit to achieve maximum efficiency.
