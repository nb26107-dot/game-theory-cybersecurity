# 🟢 Chapter 1.4: Game Theory under Uncertainty and Incomplete Information

Real-world defenders operate in a perpetual "fog of war." We rarely know the exact capabilities of our adversaries, what zero-days they hold, or when they will strike. This is known as a game of **Incomplete Information**.

To model this, we use **Bayesian Game Theory**, pioneered by John C. Harsanyi.

---

## 🎭 1. The Concept of "Types" and Harsanyi's Framework

In a Bayesian game, our uncertainty about the adversary is modeled by categorizing them into different **"Types"**. Since we cannot see their true type, we use **Beliefs (Probabilities)** derived from Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI).

Mathematically, the game introduces a fictional player called **"Nature"** who randomly selects the attacker's type at the start, keeping it hidden from the defender:

* **Type A: High-Capability Attacker (APT):** Armed with zero-day exploits, highly persistent, and well-funded.
* **Type B: Low-Capability Attacker (Script Kiddie):** Relies on public open-source tools; easily deterred by minor security hurdles.

The defender must calculate their defensive strategies based on the **Expected Payoff** of handling a combination of these probabilities.

---

## 📡 2. Signaling Games and Cyber Deception

When information is incomplete, both sides look for **"Signals"** to guess the opponent's true state. Defenders can actively manipulate these signals using **Cyber Deception** (e.g., Honeypots and Canary Tokens).

Attackers scan the network asking: *"Is this target vulnerable or secure?"*

### 🛠️ Defensive Outcomes:
1. **Separating Equilibrium:** The defender sets up a simulated, highly vulnerable port (a Honeypot). A low-capability attacker leaps at it and gets caught. An APT attacker gets suspicious of how "easy" it looks and retreats. The signal successfully separates the attacker types.
2. **Pooling Equilibrium:** The defender disguises a highly critical production server to look exactly like an ordinary machine or a decoy honeypot. The attacker hesitates to use their valuable zero-day exploit, fearing it might be wasted on a trap.

In a state of uncertainty, **delaying an attacker's movement through calculated doubt is a major tactical victory for the Blue Team.**

## 🏛️ References & Academic Literature
* **Harsanyi, J. C. (1967) - *Games with Incomplete Information Played by 'Bayesian' Players*:** Nobel-prize-winning framework for modeling uncertainty.
* **Carroll, T. E., & Grosu, D. (2011) - *A Game Theoretic Investigation of Deception in Network Security*:** An IEEE paper analyzing Signaling Games mathematically to prove the tactical value of Honeypots against asymmetric adversaries.
